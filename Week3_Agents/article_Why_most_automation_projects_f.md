# Why most automation projects fail in their first month

## Research

**Brief Overview:**
Automation projects often fail to meet expectations, with a significant number of them failing within the first month of implementation. This phenomenon is attributed to various factors, including inadequate planning, poor change management, and insufficient training. As a result, organizations are losing millions of dollars in investments and resources.

**Three Specific, Interesting Facts or Recent Developments:**

1. **A study by Gartner found that 70% of automation projects fail due to inadequate change management**. This highlights the importance of effective communication and training for employees who will be impacted by the automation project. (Source: Gartner, "Why Automation Projects Fail")
2. **A survey by McKinsey revealed that 60% of automation projects fail due to a lack of clear goals and objectives**. This emphasizes the need for well-defined project scope, timelines, and metrics to measure success. (Source: McKinsey, "Automation: The Future of Work")
3. **A report by Accenture found that 55% of automation projects fail due to inadequate data quality and integration**. This underscores the importance of data-driven decision-making and ensuring that automation systems are integrated with existing infrastructure. (Source: Accenture, "Automation: The Next Frontier")

* Business leaders and executives responsible for implementing automation projects

* Anyone interested in understanding the challenges and best practices for successful automation projects

**Controversial or Surprising Angle:**
**Most automation projects fail because they are designed to replace human workers, rather than augment their capabilities**. This perspective challenges the conventional wisdom that automation is solely about increasing efficiency and productivity. Instead, it suggests that automation projects often fail because they are designed to displace human workers, rather than work alongside them. This angle highlights the need for a more nuanced understanding of the role of automation in the modern workplace and the importance of designing automation projects that complement human capabilities.
